## How to Read This

The Phase 3 research validated (or invalidated) Phase 2 heuristic scores using 400+ web searches across 5 areas per trade. Rather than applying rigid pass/fail cutoffs, the top 10 are ranked by corrected total score with Big 4 sub-scores noted. Every trade has real strengths worth discussing — the question is which combination of strengths best fits the startup model.

Phase 2 heuristic scoring systematically overestimated three things:

1. **Competition whitespace** — Trades that appeared "niche" in Phase 2 turned out to have established franchise and/or national competitors already running the in-home-selling model (closets, bath conversions, cabinet refacing, garage flooring, outdoor lighting)
2. **Job size viability** — 20 of 27 trades have average tickets below $10K, far under the $15K-$25K sweet spot required for in-home-selling economics (salesperson commissions + marketing + overhead)
3. **Niche vs. general contracting** — Several "niche" trades (garage gym, home office build-out) dissolved into general remodeling on closer inspection, requiring GC licenses, multiple sub trades, and multi-week timelines

## Cross-Cutting Themes

### 1. Job Size Is the Structural Killer

20 of 27 trades have average tickets below $10K. The in-home-selling model requires $15K-$25K to cover: salesperson commissions (8-12%), marketing/lead gen (15-20%), crew costs, overhead, and profit. At $5K-$8K average tickets, the math doesn't work regardless of how good the other dimensions are.

**Implication:** Either the business model needs to accommodate lower tickets (higher volume, lower sales cost), or the trade selection needs to focus on $15K+ jobs (which typically means more complex projects with longer timelines — creating a different kind of challenge).

### 2. Phase 2 Systematically Underestimated Franchise Competition

At least 8 trades had established national franchise competitors running the exact in-home-selling model that weren't captured in Phase 2 heuristic scoring: closets (5+ franchises), bath conversions (6+ franchises), cabinet refacing (N-Hance), garage flooring (3+ franchises), outdoor lighting (3+ franchises), garage organization (Monkey Bars), custom shelving (5+ franchises), and home automation (Vivint).

**Implication:** The Wasatch Front in-home-selling market is more mature than expected. The best opportunity may be in trades where NO franchise has yet deployed this model.

### 3. "Niche" Trades Often Dissolve Into General Contracting

Garage gym build-out, home office build-out, and to some extent sauna installation look like targeted niches on paper but require general contractor licenses, multiple sub trades, and multi-week timelines in practice. The niche appeal is real for marketing, but operations are standard GC work.

**Implication:** True operational niches (1-2 day installs, 2-person crews, single trade) that also have $15K+ tickets are extremely rare. This may be the fundamental tension in the scoring rubric.

### 4. Bundling May Be the Path Forward

Several trades with strong individual attributes (excellent margins, fast installs, low competition) fail only on ticket size. Epoxy flooring (60% margins, 1-day install), garage organization, accent walls, outdoor lighting, and sauna add-ons (cold plunge, etc.) all have this profile. A bundled multi-trade offering — e.g., "Total Garage Transformation" (epoxy + organization + gym features) — could potentially create a $15K-$25K composite ticket.
