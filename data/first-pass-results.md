## Surprise Check

### Surprisingly Low: Insulation — Spray Foam

**Expected:** Mid-range Consideration (~270–290). **Actual:** 288, Big 4: 104 — REJECTED by 1 point.

Spray foam has excellent operational characteristics — fast 1-day installs, 2-person crew, low callbacks, year-round work, growing energy efficiency demand. Expected it to comfortably make the Consideration list. The surprise is that the Big 4 economics barely miss: average job size of $4K–$15K (score 2) and moderate competition (score 3) create a Big 4 of exactly 104. This is the closest rejection in the entire set and could flip with better competition data from Wasatch Front research.

### Surprisingly High: Garage Gym Build-out

**Expected:** Low-to-mid Consideration (~270–290) as a niche brainstormed trade. **Actual:** 351 — highest score in the entire set.

Expected this to be a moderate niche trade, but the scoring math tells a different story. Zero competition (5), combined with operationally ideal characteristics (1-day install, 2-person crew, no permits, no callbacks), created a perfect storm for high scores. The fundamental question is market depth — can you build a $500K–$1M business on garage gym build-outs alone? Phase 3 should investigate addressable market size on the Wasatch Front.

### Most Surprising: Faux Painting & Decorative Finishes — 311

**Expected:** Low Consideration (~240–260) given the artisan labor challenge. **Actual:** 311 with the second-highest Big 4 (148/175).

This was the most counterintuitive result. A trade where the labor score is 1 (the worst possible) scored 311 overall and had the second-best Big 4 economics. The math reveals why: Labor Skill Level carries weight 6 (only 6 points impact from score 1 vs 30 points if it were 5), while Competition carries weight 10 (50 points from score 5). The rubric implicitly says "if no one else can do it either, the labor barrier IS your competitive moat." Whether that's actually true or just a scoring artifact is a critical Phase 3 question — can you actually find and train Venetian plaster artisans in Utah?
