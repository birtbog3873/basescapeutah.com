#### Research Findings

**Local Competition:** The Utah battery storage market is more competitive than Phase 2 assumed. ES Solar is the dominant force — they've partnered with Sonnen to retrofit 200-500 homes per month through Rocky Mountain Power's Wattsmart program, targeting 32,000 customers over 5 years. Additional active installers include SunRidge Solar, Elite Electric, Intermountain Wind & Solar, and Go Solar. SolarEdge is now assembling residential batteries in Salt Lake City. While no single franchise dominates, ES Solar's Wattsmart partnership gives them a scale advantage that would be difficult for a new entrant to overcome without a similar utility relationship.

**Job Size & Pricing:** Home battery storage systems cost $9,000-$18,000 installed before incentives, with a typical 10 kWh system at $8,000-$11,000. The Wattsmart program provides a $2,000 upfront incentive per household (capped at $400/kW). After the Wattsmart rebate, customer cost is $6,000-$16,000. This broadly aligns with the $15K-$25K sweet spot at the higher end, but the average residential battery-only installation (without solar) lands in the $8K-$14K range — below the sweet spot for many customers.

**Margins:** Battery storage installer margins are challenging. Equipment costs represent 50-60% of the installed price, with soft costs (permitting, interconnection, customer acquisition) consuming another 17-20%. Industry data shows typical solar/battery dealers have cost-to-install of 80% of revenue, with customer acquisition at 17% — leaving roughly 3% net profit. Manufacturer gross margins are 20-30%, but installer margins are structurally compressed. Without the 30% IRA tax credit inflating the sticker price that customers tolerate, margin pressure will intensify in 2026+.

**Local Demand & Utah Factors:** Rocky Mountain Power's Wattsmart Battery program is a genuine Utah-specific demand driver — it was recognized by the DOE as a "best-in-class" virtual power plant. The program provides upfront incentives and ongoing bill credits in exchange for grid access during peak demand. Utah's rapidly growing housing stock and increasing electricity costs support demand. However, the 30% IRA residential clean energy tax credit expired December 31, 2025 with no extension — this removes the largest financial incentive for homeowners. The "One Big Beautiful Bill" accelerated the phase-out of nearly all residential clean energy credits. National residential battery market growth remains strong (17.9% CAGR, 50%+ capacity growth in 2025), but this growth was heavily subsidized by the now-expired federal credit.

**Licensing & Permits:** Battery storage installation in Utah requires an S202 Solar PV Contractor License from DOPL, which mandates NABCEP certification, 2 years of experience under a licensed contractor, and passing both Law & Business and trade-specific exams. Electrical connections must be performed by an S200 or S201 licensed electrical contractor. Installation requires electrical permits, fire code compliance (Utah Fire Code Chapter 12), AHJ inspection of wiring/mounting/safety clearances, and utility interconnection approval. The permitting and licensing burden is among the heaviest of any residential trade.

#### Key Insight
> The IRA tax credit expiration fundamentally changes the economics of standalone battery storage for new market entrants. ES Solar's first-mover advantage with Wattsmart, combined with licensed-trade barriers and multi-month sales cycles, makes this trade incompatible with the independent in-home-selling startup model — even though the underlying demand is real.

**Status: DROPPED** — Score fell from 277 to 246 (needs 290). Big 4 fell from 116 to 106 (needs 122). Three fatal flaws: (1) IRA tax credit expiration removes the primary consumer purchase motivator, (2) 2-4 month sales-to-activation timeline is incompatible with one-call-close model, (3) S202/NABCEP licensing requirement and ~3% net margins make bootstrapping under $100K startup capital impractical. ES Solar's dominant position in the Wattsmart channel further narrows the competitive lane.

#### Research Findings

**Local Competition:** The Wasatch Front has 7-10+ concrete contractors offering decorative services: SLC Concrete Co, KFM Construction (20+ years, specializes in stamped/stained), Aloha Concrete, Legend Construction, Masiva Builders, JCR General Contractor (10+ years stamped), and others. Critically, these are traditional trade contractors — none operates an in-home-selling model with appointment setters and consultants. The market also has adjacent coating companies (Utah Epoxy Coatings, M3 Concrete Coatings, Hermetic Coatings, Elite Concrete Coatings, Invictus Coatings) and one decorative franchise (Concrete Craft). Competition is real but fragmented among small operators with no dominant brand.

**Job Size & Pricing:** Stamped concrete runs $9-$22/sqft installed, with basic patterns at $8-$12/sqft and complex multi-color designs at $20-$28/sqft. A typical 20'x20' stamped patio costs $3,200-$7,600. A 2-car stamped driveway costs $6,900-$10,400. Combined patio + driveway projects can reach $10K-$15K. To hit the $15K-$25K sweet spot, you'd need large-scope projects (600+ sqft patio + driveway + walkways with premium finishes). Achievable but not the average job — most residential decorative concrete projects fall in the $5K-$10K range.

**Margins:** Decorative concrete commands higher margins than plain flatwork, typically 25-35% gross margin with 40% considered relatively high. Material costs for decorative are modest — concrete, color hardener, release agents, stamps, and sealers. Labor is the primary cost driver at $5-$15/sqft depending on complexity. Standard contractor markup is 30-50% on materials and labor. Net profit for concrete contractors is 10-25% on decorative work versus 10-15% on commodity flatwork. The margin profile supports the business model but doesn't reach the 55%+ threshold for a score of 5.

**Local Demand & Utah Factors:** Utah is among the top growth states for single-family housing construction, directly driving demand for driveways, patios, and outdoor living spaces. The global decorative concrete market is growing at 5.4-5.9% CAGR, with the residential segment at 6.5% CAGR. Stamped concrete holds 40%+ of decorative concrete market share. Utah's large family sizes drive demand for outdoor entertainment areas. However, Utah's extreme freeze-thaw cycles (frequent temperature swings across 32°F) create a real durability concern for stamped concrete — water expanding 9% when freezing causes internal cracking, and road salt accelerates degradation. Proper air entrainment (4-7%) and biennial resealing mitigate this, but it remains a post-sale risk factor.

**Licensing & Permits:** Decorative concrete flatwork falls under the S260 Asphalt & Concrete specialty contractor license from DOPL. Requirements include relevant experience, passing written exams, general liability insurance ($100K per incident / $300K total), workers' compensation insurance, state tax registration, and unemployment registration. Compared to electrical or plumbing trades, the licensing burden is moderate. Building permits are required for concrete flatwork but the process is straightforward — no complex multi-agency inspections. No electrical or mechanical inspections needed.

#### Key Insight
> Decorative concrete has the strongest Big 4 sub-score (129) of any triage survivor and genuine differentiation potential via visual in-home selling. But the average job size of $5K-$10K is the structural problem — reaching the $15K-$25K sweet spot requires large combined-scope projects that won't be the norm. The 7-8 month selling season in Utah further constrains Year 1 revenue potential.

**Status: DROPPED** — Total score 281 (needs 290), falling 9 points short despite Big 4 passing at 129/175. The gap is driven by non-Big-4 weaknesses: freeze-thaw post-sale risk (dropped to 2), 7-8 month seasonality (dropped to 2), and stubborn labor skill constraints. The $5K-$10K average job size, while not fatal on its own, means hitting $500K-$1M Year 1 revenue requires 50-100+ completed jobs in a 7-8 month window — roughly 7-12 jobs per month — which is aggressive for a startup crew navigating Utah's construction labor shortage. Decorative concrete is a legitimate trade opportunity for someone already in the concrete business, but it doesn't clear the bar for a de novo in-home-selling startup.
