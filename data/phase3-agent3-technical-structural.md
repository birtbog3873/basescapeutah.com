#### Research Findings

**Local Competition:** The Wasatch Front shower/bath conversion market is one of the most fiercely competed in-home-sales trades in Utah. At least 7-8 dedicated competitors operate with the exact appointment-setter → in-home-consultant → one-call-close model: Re-Bath (national franchise), Bath Fitter (national franchise, operated by BILVAN LLC in SLC), West Shore Home (40+ branches nationally, W2 employees), KOHLER by Renuity (brand-name partnership), Five Star Bath Solutions (franchise), One Day Bath (Jacuzzi dealer), Bathcrest (local, since 1979), and Lifespan Home Improvement. An independent startup entering this market would face entrenched national brands with massive ad budgets and optimized supply chains.

**Job Size & Pricing:** Basic tub-to-shower conversion: $1,500-$8,000 (average ~$3,000). Walk-in shower with upgrades: $6,000-$12,000. Mid-range with wall panels and glass: $8,000-$15,000. Custom tiled conversions: $3,500-$15,000. The typical sale through franchise channels likely lands at $8K-$12K with upsells, which is below the ideal $15K-$25K sweet spot. (Sources: Angi, HomeAdvisor, Zintex, HomeGuide)

**Margins:** Strong. Re-Bath franchise owners averaged 51.3% gross margin in 2024. Specialty tub-to-shower operations see 35-50% gross margins. This is driven by acrylic liner/panel systems with low material cost relative to selling price. However, an independent startup without franchise supply chain advantages would likely achieve the lower end (35-45%). (Sources: Re-Bath FDD, Innovate Building Solutions, Leap)

**Local Demand & Utah Factors:** Bathroom remodeling is a large, steady market. Utah's 1970s-1990s housing stock provides a renovation base. However, the market is well-served — six+ competitors are already capturing this demand with aggressive marketing. Utah's aging 65+ population grew 53% between 2010-2020 (Kem C. Gardner Policy Institute), creating accessibility-driven demand, but competitors have already positioned for this with walk-in tubs and accessible showers.

**Licensing & Permits:** Plumbing work requires a licensed master plumber (P200 classification through Utah DOPL). The R100 residential contractor license does NOT cover plumbing, electrical, or mechanical work. Building permits required when modifying plumbing lines. This means the business needs either a licensed plumber on staff or a reliable plumbing subcontractor relationship. (Source: Utah DOPL)

#### Key Insight
> The shower conversion trade has excellent margins (50%+) and operational simplicity (1-day installs), but the Wasatch Front market is dominated by 6+ national franchise competitors all running the exact in-home-sales model this startup would use. Entering this market would mean competing head-to-head against Re-Bath, Bath Fitter, and West Shore Home on their home turf — not a good use of Steven's marketing edge.

#### Research Findings

**Local Competition:** The Wasatch Front weatherization/insulation market is fragmented with mostly small independent contractors. Key players: Elite Energy Solutions (est. 2009, high-rated by utility weatherization programs), Energy Doctors (Northern Utah, insulation focus), iFOAM of Layton (spray foam franchise). Government programs serve low-income homeowners through Utah Community Action and the WAP program but don't compete for private-pay customers. No national franchise uses an in-home-selling model for weatherization — this is a genuinely low-competition space.

**Job Size & Pricing:** Average insulation job: $1,600-$8,000. Attic insulation (1,500 sq ft): $2,100-$4,875. Spray foam whole-house: $5,000-$10,000. Comprehensive whole-house weatherization packages: $3,000-$7,000 typical. Even the most aggressive bundling (air sealing + attic insulation + wall insulation + duct sealing) rarely exceeds $10K-$14K. This fundamentally fails the in-home-selling economics test — the job size can't support salesperson commissions, marketing costs, and overhead. (Sources: HomeGuide, HomeAdvisor, DOE)

**Margins:** Spray foam contractors report 40-50% gross margins with established operations (Intech Equipment, Profoam). A single spray foam rig with 2-person crew can generate $500K-$1M annually. However, materials represent 50-60% of spray foam costs, and blown-in insulation is even more material-heavy. Blended gross margin for a full-service weatherization business: 35-45%.

**Local Demand & Utah Factors:** Utah's hot dry summers and cold winters create genuine weatherization need. Utility rebate programs (Enbridge ThermWise) incentivize work and create referral pipelines. Utah's 1970s-1990s housing stock often has inadequate insulation. However, the strong DIY culture in Utah means many homeowners tackle basic weatherization themselves. Federal IRA incentives add demand tailwind. (Sources: Enbridge Gas, DOE)

**Licensing & Permits:** S250 Insulation Contractor license required through Utah DOPL. 25-hour pre-licensure course required from ABC, UHBA, or AGC. General liability insurance minimum $100K/$300K. Most weatherization work does NOT require building permits. If HVAC ductwork is involved, separate HVAC license needed. Relatively straightforward licensing path. (Source: Utah DOPL)

#### Key Insight
> Weatherization has favorable competition dynamics (no national in-home-sales franchises) and fast installation times, but the fundamental problem is job size: average projects of $3K-$8K cannot support the in-home-selling model's cost structure (salesperson commissions, marketing spend, overhead). This trade would need to be bundled into larger packages to work, and even then it's hard to reach $15K.

#### Research Findings

**Local Competition:** The Wasatch Front has 7-8 established basement waterproofing/foundation repair companies. (Sources: HomeBlue, Angi, HomeAdvisor, Basement Waterproofing Scientists)

**Margins:** Specialized foundation/waterproofing contractors target 40-60% gross margins (Financial Models Lab benchmarks). Labor accounts for 50-70% of crawl space encapsulation costs. The specialized, problem-solving nature of the work supports premium pricing — homeowners with water problems pay urgently. (Sources: Financial Models Lab, Polyguard, Service Titan)

**Local Demand & Utah Factors:** Utah's semi-arid climate means basement water issues are less prevalent than in the Midwest or Southeast. However, snowmelt runoff in spring creates seasonal demand, and some valley-floor areas have high water tables. The real opportunity may be moisture prevention/vapor barrier work rather than emergency waterproofing.

**Licensing & Permits:** S310 Foundation, Excavation, and Demolition Contractor license required through Utah DOPL for foundation-related work. Interior waterproofing (drain systems, sump pumps, vapor barriers) may fall under general contractor or specialty classifications. General liability insurance minimum $100K/$300K required. Most interior waterproofing work does not require building permits. Exterior excavation may require permits. (Source: Utah DOPL)

#### Key Insight
> Basement waterproofing has decent margins (40-50%) and moderate competition, but the combination of below-sweet-spot job sizes ($5K-$10K average), significant post-sale warranty risk (lifetime warranties on water control systems), and Utah's relatively dry climate make this a marginal fit. The Big 4 score of 121 is tantalizingly close to the 122 threshold but doesn't clear it — and conservative scoring of the job size dimension (research confirmed Ph2 was optimistic) pushed it further below.

#### Research Findings

**Local Competition:** The Wasatch Front has almost no dedicated home accessibility modification companies. Age-Proof Homes operates in Southern Utah (not the Wasatch Front). Live in Place Designs focuses on design consulting. Lifeway Mobility and 101 Mobility sell stairlifts but don't offer comprehensive home modifications. This is a genuinely underserved market with an enormous demand tailwind — but the business economics don't support the in-home-selling model.

**Margins:** Home modification services: 20-30% profit margins (Wealth Formula). Stairlifts are resale items with dealer margins of 30-40% but require manufacturer training and warranty support. Grab bars and ramps have better margins (50%+ for materials + labor) but are tiny tickets. Bathroom accessibility renovations follow remodeling margins (35-45%). Blended margin across modification types: likely 25-35%, below the 40%+ needed for the in-home-selling model. (Sources: Wealth Formula, Handyman Startup)

**Local Demand & Utah Factors:** Exceptional demographic tailwind. Utah's 65+ population grew 53% between 2010-2020 (Gardner Policy Institute), and is projected to make up nearly 25% of population by 2060 (currently ~12%). However, Utah's culture of family care (adult children help aging parents) and large household sizes may reduce demand for formal accessibility services. Medicare and VA funding programs exist but create billing complexity. Salt Lake, Davis, and Utah counties have the smallest share of 65+ residents but the largest absolute numbers.

**Licensing & Permits:** Most accessibility modifications under $3,000 can be done under the Utah handyman exemption (DOPL registration, $35 fee). Plumbing, electrical, and HVAC work always requires licensed tradespersons regardless of project size. Stairlift installation may require electrical permit. #### Research Findings

**Local Competition:** Remarkably low. Wasatch Walkout Basements (wasatchwalkouts.com) bills itself as "Utah's first and only walkout basement specialist" — and the research largely confirms this. #### Key Insight
> Basement walkouts have the strongest job size alignment ($15K-$60K) and lowest competition of any trade in this batch — Wasatch Walkout Basements is literally the only dedicated specialist in the state. The ADU/rental income narrative is a powerful sales lever in Utah's housing market. This trade could work as a second or third business line once the company has established subcontractor relationships, capital reserves, and operational maturity — but it's not a Year 1 launch trade for a lean startup using the in-home-selling model.
