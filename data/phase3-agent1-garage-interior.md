#### Research Findings

**Local Competition:** No dedicated "garage gym build-out" company uses an in-home-selling model on the Wasatch Front. However, multiple general contractors serve this niche: Pro-Worx Construction (explicitly markets "Home Gym Remodels & Fitness Room Renovations Utah"), Salt Lake Custom Builders (garage conversions including gyms), Dave Johnson Construction (custom garage conversions), and Equity Builders (20+ years in garage construction). The competitive landscape is better described as "general remodeling contractors who include gym conversions in their service menu" rather than a white-space niche. A new entrant would compete against established GCs with broader service portfolios and existing referral networks.

**Job Size & Pricing:** Basic garage gym setup (rubber flooring, basic insulation, lighting): $8,000-$12,000. Advanced builds with HVAC, mirrors, custom storage, rubber flooring, electrical upgrades: $15,000-$30,000+. General garage conversion (non-gym): $6,000-$27,000 (A1 Garage, This Old House, Angi 2026 data). The typical homeowner converting a garage to a gym spends $10K-$15K. Only highly customized builds reach the $15K-$25K sweet spot, and those projects require significant GC scope. (Sources: Pro-Worx Construction, HomeAdvisor 2026, Angi 2026)

**Margins:** This is fundamentally general remodeling work. Residential remodeling average gross margins: 25-35% for small contractors, with net profit margins of 8-12% (NextInsurance, Autodesk). The "gym" specialization doesn't command a premium over standard garage conversion pricing. Material-heavy projects (insulation + HVAC + flooring) further compress margins. No evidence of specialty gym build-out companies achieving premium margins.

**Local Demand & Utah Factors:** Utah's population grew by 37,000+ on the Wasatch Front between 2023-2024, with 18,000 new housing units (Kem C. Gardner Policy Institute). Utah's active, fitness-oriented culture and large families (average household size above national average) create demand for home fitness spaces. The garage gym subculture is notably strong in Utah — Rogue Fitness featured a "Rogue Equipped Garage Gym Tour" from the Wasatch Mountains. However, this demand is dispersed across DIY projects and general contractors, not concentrated in a specialty trade.

**Licensing & Permits:** A meaningful garage gym conversion requires an R100 (Residential & Small Commercial) general contractor license from Utah DOPL, which mandates 2+ years (4,000 hours) of construction experience and a 25-hour pre-licensure course. Electrical work requires a licensed E100 subcontractor. HVAC work requires a licensed S350 subcontractor. Building permits are required when modifying electrical, HVAC, or converting to habitable space. This is NOT a low-barrier specialty trade. (Source: Utah DOPL)

#### Key Insight
> Garage gym build-out scored high in Phase 2 by assuming it was a niche specialty. Research reveals it's actually general remodeling with a fitness theme — requiring a GC license, multi-trade coordination, and 1-3 week project timelines. The niche appeal is real, but the operational complexity and licensing requirements make it a poor fit for a lean startup targeting 1-2 day installs with trainable crews.

#### Research Findings

**Local Competition:** The Wasatch Front epoxy/polyaspartic market is competitive with 8-10+ active operators. Several use free on-site estimate models. This is NOT an unserved market. (Sources: Google search, company websites, Yelp, Angi)

**Job Size & Pricing:** A standard 2-car garage (400-500 sq ft) costs $1,600-$5,800 for professional epoxy coating. Polyaspartic coatings run higher: $2,000-$6,900 for the same footprint. Salt Lake City-specific pricing: $4,000-$5,500 for high-quality 2-car garage work (Craftsman Concrete Floors). Cost per square foot ranges from $4-$12 depending on system quality. Even at the premium end ($12/sqft x 500 sqft = $6,000), this falls far short of the $15K-$25K sweet spot. The average homeowner pays $2,000-$4,000. (Sources: HomeGuide 2026, Angi 2026, Barefoot Concrete Coatings Utah, Homewyse 2026)

**Margins:** Strong margin trade. Material costs: $0.50-$3 per square foot (30-40% of total). Labor: 60-70% of project cost but at low absolute dollar amounts. Gross margins of 37-60% are achievable depending on job type: standard flake coatings ~37-45%, decorative metallic finishes up to 60%. Bulk material purchasing can push margins higher. GarageExperts franchise reports average revenue of $616K against startup costs of $97K-$186K, implying strong unit economics. Profit markup of 50-100% over COGS is industry standard. (Sources: Performance Financial LLC, Wexford Insurance, GarageExperts FDD via Franchise Chatter, Barefoot Concrete Coatings)

**Local Demand & Utah Factors:** Utah's fast population growth (37K+ new Wasatch Front residents 2023-2024) and high homeownership rate create steady demand. The hot/dry summers and cold/snowy winters make garage floor coatings practical (road salt protection, moisture resistance). Utah's car culture and large garages (many homes have 3-car garages) expand the addressable market. Floor coatings market growing at 4.5-6.9% CAGR nationally. However, the market is already well-served by multiple competitors. (Sources: Kem C. Gardner Policy Institute, Grand View Research)

**Licensing & Permits:** Floor coating work falls under Utah S300 (General Painting Contractor) specialty license. Requirements: 25-hour pre-licensure course, $100K/$300K general liability insurance, workers' comp, state tax registration. No experience requirement for specialty contractor license. No building permits needed for floor coating. This is one of the lowest-barrier trades in terms of licensing. (Source: Utah DOPL)

#### Key Insight
> Epoxy/polyaspartic garage flooring has excellent margins (40-60%), simple operations (2-person crew, 1-day installs), and low licensing barriers. However, the fatal flaw for this business model is job size: the average residential project is $2,000-$5,500 — far too low for in-home-selling economics where appointment-setting, sales consultant compensation, and financing overhead need $15K+ tickets to pencil out. This trade works brilliantly as a component of a larger garage makeover bundle, but struggles to stand alone as an in-home-sales business.

> Accent wall installation has the lowest competition of any trade in this batch, but that's because the market fundamentally can't support dedicated in-home-selling businesses — jobs average $500-$2,500 per wall, yielding only $250-$1,250 gross profit each. The per-job economics are incompatible with appointment-setter → in-home-consultant → one-call-close overhead. This is a painter's or carpenter's add-on service, not a standalone business.

### Key Themes Across Batch 1

1. **Job Size is the recurring killer.** Five of six trades have average tickets well below $15K-$25K: epoxy flooring ($2K-$5.5K), garage storage ($1.5K-$5K), closet systems ($2K-$7.5K), accent walls ($500-$2.5K). Even garage gym and home office build-outs cluster at $8K-$15K median.

2. **"Niche" trades dissolve into general remodeling.** Garage gym build-out and home office build-out scored high in Phase 2 as "niche specialties" but research reveals they're standard general contracting projects requiring R100 licenses, multi-trade coordination, and multi-week timelines.

3. **Franchise saturation in organization trades.** Closet organization (5+ national franchises) and garage organization (3+ national franchises) both have significant franchise presence on the Wasatch Front, specifically using the in-home-consultation sales model.

4. **Epoxy/polyaspartic and garage storage have the strongest standalone economics** but fail on ticket size. These trades work best as components of a bundled "garage transformation" offering — a potential avenue worth exploring in Phase 4 if a viable ticket-raising strategy can be identified.

5. **Margins are genuinely strong** across most of these trades (40-60% for epoxy, 45-55% for storage, 35-50% for closets). The margin profile isn't the problem — it's the absolute dollar volume per transaction that doesn't support in-home-selling overhead.
