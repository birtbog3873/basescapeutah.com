## Overview

These three trades represent distinct competitive landscapes that, on paper, offer different value propositions for a startup: one is a proven in-home-sales model (shower conversion) with entrenched franchise competition; another targets a premium outdoor living trend (outdoor kitchen) with complex operations; and the third operates in a low-competition space (weatherization) but with fundamentally small tickets.

**The bottom line up front:** None of these three trades is a strong standalone fit for the in-home-selling model. Shower conversion is a franchise battleground. Outdoor kitchens have margin and timeline problems. Weatherization can't generate enough revenue per sale. However, each reveals something useful about where the real opportunity might be — and the complementary trade analysis at the end surfaces combinations worth considering.

#### Customer Complaints (Common Themes)

Research across Google Reviews, Yelp, BBB, PissedConsumer, and Trustpilot reveals recurring complaint patterns:

- **Re-Bath Utah (BBB A+ rating, 3 complaints in 3 years):** Delivery timeline issues — doors promised quickly but arriving weeks late. Installation fit problems — tubs not fitting through doors, requiring rerouted water lines. Wall paint damage during installation not repaired.
- **Bath Fitter Utah (Yelp 3 stars, 17 reviews):** "Promises they didn't keep" and "very rude staff" are recurring themes. National BBB profile shows 500+ complaints in 3 years — installation quality and pushy sales tactics dominate.
- **West Shore Home (BBB A rating):** Fewer complaints than Bath Fitter nationally. 4/5 stars from 5,000+ reviews. Strongest reputation of the major players.
- **Industry-wide pattern:** High-pressure one-call-close tactics generate buyer's remorse complaints. Installation quality varies by individual crew. Post-sale communication is weak across most players.

#### In-Home Selling Model

Every major competitor operates the exact appointment-setter → in-home-consultant → one-call-close model. This is arguably the single most competed in-home-sales trade on the Wasatch Front. Re-Bath alone averages $4.25M per franchise unit nationally with 51.3% gross margins — these are well-funded, well-optimized operations.

#### Market Gaps

Despite the saturation, gaps exist:
1. **Price transparency:** Customers consistently complain about opaque pricing. Nobody publishes prices. An independent could differentiate with transparent, published pricing tiers.
2. **Post-sale communication:** Most competitors go silent between sale and install day. Proactive updates would stand out.
3. **Design flexibility:** Franchise systems use proprietary acrylic systems with limited design options. A custom tile option with one-week (not one-day) install could serve the design-conscious segment.
4. **Mid-market gap:** Franchises push $8K-$15K acrylic conversions. Homeowners who want better-than-acrylic but not a $25K+ full remodel have few options.

**Assessment:** The gaps are real but narrow. An independent startup would need to out-execute 8-10 well-funded competitors on their home turf. Steven's marketing skills would be neutralized by the franchise ad budgets — Re-Bath, West Shore Home, and Bath Fitter collectively spend millions on TV, digital, and direct mail in the SLC market.

### Dimension 6: Differentiation Playbook

**Branded offer concept: "WalkIn Pro — The Transparent Bath Conversion"**

The differentiation strategy here must exploit franchise weaknesses:

1. **Published pricing tiers** — No other competitor publishes prices. Create 3 clear packages (Good/Better/Best at $6K/$9K/$13K) on the website. This builds trust and pre-qualifies leads before the in-home visit.

2. **Warranty structure:**
   - Lifetime warranty on acrylic panels and installation (matches franchises)
   - 5-year "No-Leak" guarantee on all plumbing connections
   - 2-year "Love It" satisfaction guarantee — if not satisfied, full rework at no cost

3. **Add-on menu:**
   | Add-on | Price Range | Natural Fit |
   |--------|------------|-------------|
   | Glass door upgrade (frameless) | +$800-$2,000 | Every conversion |
   | Grab bar package (ADA) | +$200-$500 | Aging-in-place buyers |
   | Second bathroom conversion | +$6K-$10K | Whole-home approach |
   | Tile accent wall (vs. full acrylic) | +$1,000-$2,500 | Design-conscious buyers |
   | Rain showerhead + body jets | +$300-$800 | Luxury upgrade |

4. **Customer experience differentiators:**
   - Pre-install video walkthrough of exactly what will happen
   - Real-time install photos sent to homeowner's phone
   - 30-day post-install check-in call
   - Branded "before/after" photo book left with homeowner

5. **Guarantee: "Price Match + 10%"** — If any competitor offers the same scope of work for less, match and beat by 10%. Forces transparency.

**Honest assessment:** These differentiators are solid but incremental. They don't fundamentally change the competitive dynamic. The franchises have decades of brand equity, millions in ad spend, and proprietary supply chains. An independent startup would be playing catch-up, not leapfrogging.

2. **Design visualization** — Most landscape contractors don't bring 3D rendering capability to the sales visit.
3. **Appliance procurement simplification** — Homeowners currently navigate appliance selection separately from construction. A bundled approach reduces friction.
4. **Winter selling season** — Nobody actively sells outdoor kitchens October-February. But starting the build in winter means the kitchen is ready for the first spring cookout — a compelling sales pitch.

5. **Guarantee: "Save 15% or We Fix It Free"** — Measured against prior year's utility bills, adjusted for weather. This is the single strongest differentiator in the market.

**Assessment:** The differentiation potential is surprisingly strong when you approach this as a "home performance" company rather than an "insulation contractor." The free energy audit as a lead-generation and sales vehicle is particularly powerful — it creates a consultative selling experience that justifies a higher ticket price. However, the fundamental ticket size problem remains.

**What kills the business in Year 1?**
Ticket size stagnation. **Seasonal cash flow analysis:**

### The Unconventional Angle

The most interesting finding from this analysis is that **weatherization could serve as a Trojan horse for higher-ticket services.** The free energy audit gets you into 20+ homes per month. Every audit reveals not just insulation needs but also HVAC problems, crawl space issues, window efficiency losses, and comfort complaints. This creates a lead-generation engine for higher-ticket services:

- Energy audit → spray foam ($6K-$10K)
- Energy audit → crawl space encapsulation ($5K-$15K)
- Energy audit → HVAC replacement referral ($5K-$15K, referral fee)

The audit-first model is similar to how Terminix and Orkin use free home inspections to sell pest control — the inspection IS the selling tool.
