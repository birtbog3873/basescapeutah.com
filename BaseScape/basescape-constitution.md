# BaseScape Website Constitution

Version: 1.0  
Last Updated: 2026-03-21

## Preamble

This constitution governs all specifications, plans, designs, content models, and implementations for the BaseScape website and its lead-generation experiences.

Its purpose is to keep the project aligned with three realities:

1. BaseScape is selling high-trust, high-stakes structural work.
2. Prospects are often mobile, anxious, and intent-rich.
3. The website exists to create qualified conversations and booked opportunities, not to behave like a digital brochure.

All future specifications and implementation plans MUST comply with this constitution unless an exception is explicitly documented.

---

## Article I — Specialized Authority Mandate

1. BaseScape MUST be positioned as a specialist in basement walkouts, egress windows, code-compliant basement access, and ADU-enabling basement transformations.
2. The website MUST NOT present BaseScape as a generic remodeling company or broad, undifferentiated general contractor.
3. Every high-intent page MUST communicate at least one proof of specialization, such as:
   - structural foundation cutting expertise
   - drainage and moisture-control expertise
   - code-compliance fluency
   - safe excavation and site-protection practices
   - rental-income or basement-value enablement
4. Copy, visuals, and page hierarchy MUST reinforce that BaseScape solves a narrow, difficult, high-consequence problem.

## Article II — Trust Before Conversion

1. Trust signals MUST appear before or adjacent to the primary call to action on all key pages.
2. Claims MUST be supported by proof, including one or more of:
   - authentic project photography
   - before-and-after comparisons
   - testimonials or reviews
   - licensing, insurance, or permitting competence
   - clear process explanations
   - financing or rebate guidance
   - guarantees or warranties that the business can actually honor
3. Stock imagery MUST NOT serve as the primary evidence of competence on service, location, gallery, or conversion pages.
4. Each service page MUST answer the anxiety stack:
   - Is this safe?
   - Is this legal/code-compliant?
   - Will this damage my property?
   - Is it worth the money?
   - Will the crew respect my home and yard?

## Article III — Mobile-First, Speed-First Delivery

1. Mobile is the primary interface. Desktop is secondary.
2. Every critical conversion path MUST be designed for a user who is browsing quickly on a phone.
3. Core landing pages MUST target strong mobile performance, including an LCP target of 2.5 seconds or better under representative mobile conditions.
4. Client-side JavaScript MUST be minimized and used only when it materially improves conversion, usability, or essential business workflow.
5. Contact actions MUST remain easy to reach on mobile, including persistent access to primary conversion actions.

## Article IV — Clarity Before Cleverness

1. Headlines MUST lead with homeowner outcomes, not internal capabilities.
2. Copy MUST be concrete, scannable, and plainspoken.
3. Technical terms MAY be used only when they build trust or improve qualification, and they MUST be explained in simple language.
4. Every page MUST communicate:
   - what BaseScape does
   - who it is for
   - where it operates
   - what to do next
5. Clever copy that reduces comprehension is prohibited.

## Article V — Outcome-Led Messaging Framework

1. All core messaging MUST ladder to one or more of these three value pillars:
   - Financial Enablement: rental income, home value, usable square footage
   - Life Safety: egress compliance, emergency escape, family protection
   - Transformation: natural light, livable space, architectural improvement
2. Every major page MUST have one primary value pillar and one supporting proof pillar.
3. ROI, rental income, rebate, financing, appraisal, or cost-saving claims MUST be framed as estimates, ranges, or scenarios unless contractually guaranteed.
4. Fear-based messaging MAY be used only when paired with a clear, practical solution and a professional tone.

## Article VI — Compliance and Claim Integrity

1. Any statement involving codes, ADUs, permits, zoning, rebates, or legal use cases MUST be written as educational guidance, not universal legal certainty.
2. The site MUST acknowledge that city and county requirements can vary.
3. Blanket promises such as "guaranteed legal ADU" or "adds $X of value" are prohibited unless legally and factually supportable for the specific circumstance.
4. The website MUST separate:
   - what is generally true in the market
   - what BaseScape can help evaluate
   - what must be confirmed by permit review or local authority
5. High-risk claims require documented approval before publication.

## Article VII — Local Intent Architecture

1. The site MUST support service-area expansion through unique, localized pages.
2. City or area pages MUST NOT be thin duplicates with only place-name swaps.
3. Every location page MUST include localized context, proof, or relevance, such as:
   - service availability
   - local project examples
   - city-specific concerns
   - local testimonials
   - locally relevant FAQ content
4. Local pages MUST connect cleanly to services, trust signals, and conversion actions.
5. Geographic consistency across content, metadata, and business listings MUST be preserved.

## Article VIII — Lead Capture Discipline

1. Every page MUST have a clear primary conversion path.
2. The primary site-level conversion paths MUST support:
   - immediate contact for high-intent prospects
   - consultation or estimate requests for research-stage prospects
3. Forms MUST request only the information needed to qualify, route, and follow up on the lead.
4. If a form requires more than four meaningful inputs, a staged or multi-step flow SHOULD be used.
5. Form friction MUST be treated as a revenue issue, not a design preference.
6. Source attribution, service interest, and location context MUST be preserved with submitted leads wherever practical.

## Article IX — Proof-Driven Visual System

1. Real project imagery MUST be prioritized over decorative graphics.
2. Before-and-after comparisons SHOULD be presented whenever the transformation is a key selling point.
3. Visual design MUST feel premium, structured, and architecturally grounded, not like a generic contractor template.
4. Visual hierarchy MUST emphasize trust, services, proof, and conversion before decorative flourish.
5. Images and video MUST support decisions, not merely fill space.

## Article X — Structured Content and Reusable Entities

1. Content MUST be modeled as reusable entities rather than hard-coded page fragments wherever practical.
2. At minimum, the system SHOULD support structured content for:
   - services
   - service areas
   - FAQs
   - testimonials/reviews
   - case studies/projects
   - lead magnets/resources
   - offers/promotions
3. Routine marketing updates MUST be possible without developer intervention.
4. Content architecture MUST support future experimentation, paid landing pages, and location expansion without requiring a rewrite.

## Article XI — Accessibility and Inclusion

1. The experience MUST meet a WCAG 2.2 AA baseline unless a documented exception is approved.
2. Conversion-critical UI MUST provide:
   - sufficient text contrast
   - accessible labels and error states
   - keyboard accessibility
   - touch-friendly targets on mobile
3. Critical actions MUST NOT depend on hover, autoplay, or hidden gestures.
4. Accessibility defects on primary revenue paths are release-blocking defects.

## Article XII — Measurement, Attribution, and Iteration

1. The site MUST measure the full lead funnel, including as applicable:
   - phone-click events
   - form starts
   - form completions
   - resource downloads
   - booked consultations
   - landing page attribution
   - service/page-level conversion performance
2. Each core page template MUST declare its primary conversion event.
3. Experiments MUST be measurable and reversible.
4. Production learnings MUST feed back into future specifications.
5. Unmeasured conversion paths are presumed broken until proven otherwise.

## Article XIII — Simplicity and Launch Discipline

1. Version 1 MUST prioritize the fastest credible path to lead generation, trust, and usable measurement.
2. Features that do not materially improve discovery, trust, qualification, or conversion SHOULD be deferred.
3. Complexity added for speculative future needs is prohibited unless explicitly justified.
4. Launch quality is defined by clarity, speed, proof, and conversion readiness, not feature count.

---

## Default Build and Content Rules

These defaults are binding unless a later specification explicitly documents a justified exception.

1. Marketing content SHOULD be delivered through a content-first, static-first architecture.
2. Dynamic behavior SHOULD be reserved for forms, scheduling, personalization, or editor workflows where it creates clear business value.
3. Critical CTAs MUST remain available without requiring users to hunt through navigation.
4. Authentic proof assets are launch-critical dependencies, not optional enhancements.
5. Each new page type MUST identify:
   - its target audience
   - its primary value pillar
   - its primary conversion goal
   - its trust proof stack

---

## Amendment Process

Changes to this constitution require:

1. a written rationale,
2. the exact language being changed,
3. a statement of impact on existing specs and plans, and
4. approval by the project owner or designated maintainer.

If a future specification conflicts with this constitution, the constitution prevails until formally amended.
