Why Retaining Walls Can't Wait

Wasatch Front soils shift. Spring runoff, clay expansion, and freeze-thaw cycles put pressure on slopes every year. A failing hillside doesn't fix itself — it gets worse and more expensive.

Sound familiar? If you're seeing any of these, your slope is moving:
  • Fence or existing wall starting to lean
  • Cracks in your foundation, driveway, or patio
  • Water pooling against the house after rain
  • Soil creeping downhill or visible erosion channels
  • Doors or windows that stick (sign of settling)

Even one of these means your slope is actively moving. The cost to fix it only goes up.

We'll Recommend the Right Wall for Your Site

Every slope is different. During your free site assessment, we evaluate soil type, grade, and drainage to recommend the best solution — not a one-size-fits-all answer.

  • Segmental block walls — versatile, cost-effective, great for 2–6 ft heights
  • Poured concrete walls — maximum strength for tall or load-bearing applications
  • Natural stone walls — premium look with structural engineering behind it
  • Terraced systems — multi-tier solutions that turn steep slopes into usable yard

All walls over 4 feet include structural engineering stamp. Drainage systems included in every project.

Common Questions

How much does a retaining wall cost?

It depends on height, length, material, and site conditions. A short segmental block wall runs less than a tall engineered concrete wall. You'll get a fixed price after our free site visit — no vague ranges.

Do retaining walls need permits?

In most Wasatch Front cities, walls over 4 feet require a permit and structural engineering. We handle both. Even shorter walls get proper engineering when the soil or grade warrants it.

How long does construction take?

Most retaining wall projects take 1–3 weeks of on-site work, depending on length and complexity. Terraced or multi-wall systems may take longer.

What happens if I wait?

Slope erosion is progressive — every rain cycle makes it worse. A wall that costs $X today may cost 2–3X next year if the hillside reaches your foundation. The assessment is free. At minimum, you'll know what you're dealing with.
